The importance of vaccines

The COVID 19 pandemic caught the world off guard, which led to governments having to make drastic and quick choices in order to deal with the situation. Mistakes were made even with the many resources we have available today, but what can we do for the future? Right now, one of the tools we have to slow down the spread of COVID are vaccines, which have been obtained with many sacrifices and in record time. Despite the efforts made, vaccines are still the focus of a lot of controversies.

The aim

The aim of the visualization is to allow the users to be able to compare the vaccination trend for a state with other trends with the author's initial guidance, while still allowing them to explore the data in more detail and with their own perspective. With this interactive overview, it is hoped to empower the user and provide them with a tool to be able to view the pandemic from multiple perspectives in a single visualization.

Initial settings

The initial visualization settings allow the users to compare the trend in the number of people vaccinated per percent with the trend in the number of ICU patients per million since the 1st January 2020, for the G4 states (Italy, France, Germany, and the United Kingdom).

How to use

Regardless of these initial settings, users can change at their discretion which states to display, the observation period, and which measurements to compare. Likewise, they can highlight trends from one state at a time. It is also possible to merge measurements into one graph or display them in a graph per measurement. For the best experience, it is recommended to open this website from a computer or to use the device in landscape mode.

Background and Data source

The visualization was created by Pietro Lentini for the CS-E4450 - Explorative Information Visualization D course at Aalto University. The data was taken from the Our World in Data (OWID) GitHub repo. Our World in Data is a scientific online publication that focuses on large global problems and its research team is based at the University of Oxford. Data is updated as of the 11th of December 2021, but since the December data is not yet complete, it will be shown the data up to November 2021.

For further information

For official information on COVID, the World Health Organization (WHO) is an excellent source of information and data on the disease. Feel free to visit the websites for more information or to find more visualizations.
